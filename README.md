# The Demographic Timebomb: Is It Real?

This repository examines the so-called "demographic timebomb" which claims that declining birthrates and rising senior population lead to a dire situation wherein there is insufficient number of workers to support the senior population. The argument seems to be setting up a justification for reducing the government expenditure on social security and medicare. 

However, if we consider the more general class of non-workers, which includes both children and seniors, who must be financially supported by workers we find the decline in children combined with the rising number of seniors creates an altogether different picture, as shown in Figure 1.

![Demographics of non-workers vs workers](https://github.com/dchassin/demographic_timebomb/blob/main/forecast.png?raw=true)

This figure compares three scenarios with different adult fertility rates, falling by 1% per year (blue), steady (yellow), and rising by 1% per year (green). When we include children in the number of people that workers must support we find that when fertility rates decline workers support fewer people altogether.

This suggests that declining fertilility rates are not a problem overall. If there is any concern about financial support for seniors it is more likely to arise from poorly informed government policies regarding social security, senior health care, and retirement funding than it is to arise from likely outcomes of population dynamics.

# Factors Not Considered

There are a number of potential factors that are not considered in this analysis.

1. **Declining health/health care**: The model assumes a constant life-expectency for future classes of seniors. There is evidence that the generation currently aging out of the working adults class is not as healthy and not receiving the same amount of health care as the current class of seniors. This suggests that life-expectancy will decline and further reduce the number of non-workers in coming years.

2. **Accelerated fertility decline**: The model using a constant rate of change in fertilility. There is evidence that the declining fertility is accelerating. This will reduce the number of non-workers in coming years but may also reduce the number of workers in later years.

3. **Increasing child mortality**: The model assumes a constant child mortality rate. There is evidence that child mortality rates are increasing. This will further reduce the number of non-workers in coming years but may also reduce the number of workers in later years.

4. **Climate change**: The model assumes constant environment impact of overall mortality rates. There is emerging evidence current climate stressors are impacting overall mortality rates among vulnerable senior populations. This will further reduce the number of non-workers in coming years. If there are extreme climate stresses, then we might also see decreases in workers in coming years, but this is less likely than moderate climate stresses, which tend to disproportionately affect more vulnerable populations.

5. **Economic decline**: Economic boom/bust cycles correlate strongly to birth rates, child health care, and worker productivity. The next period of economic decline occurs may decrease fertility, increased mortality, and a declining ability of workers to support non-workers. However, declines are often followed by periods of growth that reverse these deliterious effects and in the long term the two more or less cancel out and we expect only a minor impact on the overall outcome.

There may be additional factors that are not discussed here. However, these factors all suggest that the model is over-estimating how many non-workers each worker must support. Taken in total, this suggests that the claim declining birth-rates are an imminent problem are incorrect and that the problem is largely insignificant and perhaps even non-existent.
